CS 6002: Selected Areas of Mechanism Design

Goal of the course

This course is based on selected topics in mechanism design. These topics include stable matching, fair allocation, cooperative games, selfish routing, games on networks, potential games. This is a research-oriented course, hence students are expected to read and present cutting-edge research topics in this area, and also develop writing skills towards a formal technical report.

A course like CS 6001 is a prerequisite for this course. In general, I will assume familiarity with formal mathematical reasoning, a fair amount of probability theory, basic calculus, the basics of computational complexity. I believe that one learns the concepts of an algorithm better when one codes that algorithm. Therefore, experience in programming will be useful.

Lectures

  • Lecture 01: One-sided Matching Problem
    The setup for one-sided matching -- also known as house allocation problem. Why this setup bypasses Gibbard-Satterthwaite? Example mechanisms: serial dictatorship, top-trading cycle. Desirable properties: strategyproofness, efficiency. Do the mechanisms satisfy these properties? Reading: [Mishra] Chapter 8. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 02: Top Trading Cycle
    TTC is strategyproof and efficient, not a serial dictatorship. Stability, core, blocking coalition. Stability properties of TTC. Reading: [Mishra] Chapter 8. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 03: Kidney Exchanges
    An application of house allocation in the organ donation market. Kidney exchange mechanisms: advantages and disadvantages of TTC. Newer mechanisms based on graph matching. Altruistic donors. Reading: Kidney Paired Donation, House Allocation with Existing Tenants, House allocation with existing tenants: A characterization, Pairwise kidney exchange. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 04: Two-sided Matching
    Blocking pairs, Gale-Shapley deferred acceptance algorithm and its properties. Stability properties: pairwise and group blocking. Structure of stable matchings -- order over the stable matchings from the men and women sides. Reading: [Mishra] Chapter 8. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 05: Structures of Stable Matchings
    Achievability of a partner, men/women-optimal functions are matchings, DA algorithm if men/women optimal, consensus among men results in reverse consensus among women over stable matchings, generalization of the pointing function idea to get lattice-type results in stable matchings. Reading: Book on "Online and Matching-Based Market Design", by Federico Echenique, Nicole Immorlica and Vijay V. Vazirani. chapters on one-sided matching, two-sided matching. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 06: Manipulation in Stable Matchings
    Stable roommates problem and Irving's algorithm. Strategyproofness in matching. DA algorithm is manipulable for the non-proposers, strategyproof for proposers. Structure of optimal manipulation and manipulation complexity. Stability question after manipulation. Readings: Stable roommates paper (Irving 1985), Stable roommates algorithm video, DA is proposer strategyproof (Dubins and Freedman 1981), structure of optimal manipulation and stability (Vaish and Garg 2017). [Handwritten notes] [Optional reading on stability and strategyproofness]
  • Lecture 07: Cake Cutting
    Allocation of a single divisible good, cake model: heterogeneous, divisible, non-identical preferred good, agent model: additive valuation, divisibility, normalization, fairness notions: proportionality, envy-freeness, Robertson-Webb query model for complexity, mechanisms: I-cut-you-choose (2 players), Dubins-Spanier algorithm (n players), Even-Paz algorithm (n players, recursive). Readings: [COMSOC] Chapter 13. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 08: Indivisible Item Allocation
    Cake cutting envy-freeness question: Selfridge-Conway algorithm for EF allocation of a cake between three agents. Upper bound for n agents [Aziz and Mackenzie, FOCS 2016], lower bound for n agents [Procaccia, IJCAI 2009]. [Wikipedia article on envy-free cake cutting], [a general audience piece]. Fair allocation of indivisible goods, envy-freeness upto one good, round-robin algorithm for additive valuations. Reading (indivisible goods): survey by Amantidis et al. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 09: Envy-freeness and Pareto Optimality
    Envy cycle elimination algorithm for monotone valuations, Pareto optimality of an allocation. Reading: Wikipedia page on Envy cycle elimination, Lipton et al. paper. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 10: EF1 and World of 'Bad's
    Pareto improvement of an EF1 allocation, Nash social welfare, Nash optimal allocation satisfies EF1 and PO. Fair allocation of Chores/Bads -- additive valuations, monotone valuations. Reading: Maximum Nash Social Welfare (NSW) paper, Maximizing NSW is APX-hard, Pseudopoly-time algorithm for EF1+PO. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 11: Bads and Mixed Items
    Monotone chores, top-trading envy-cycle elimination algorithm, mixed valuations (goods and chores), doubly round robin algorithm, doubly monotone valuations, envy-cycle top-trading envy-cycle elimination algorithm, summary. Reading: Top-trading envy cycle elimination paper, doubly round-robin algorithm. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 12: Bargaining Games
    Axioms of two-player bargaining, Nash bargaining solution, existence, and uniqueness. Reading: [MSZ] Chapter 15. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 13: Transferable Utility Games
    Characteristic function, examples of TU games, special classes of TU games. Reading: [Myer] Chapter 9. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 14: Core of TU Games
    Solution concepts of TU games, core, Bondareva-Shapley theorem. Reading: [MSZ] Chapter 17. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 15: Market Games
    Market games, non-emptiness of core, Shapley-Shubik theorem. Reading: [MSZ] Chapter 17. [Handwritten notes]
  • Lecture 16: Limitations of Core
    Multiple solutions in core, single-valued solution concepts, Shapley value, Shapley axioms, Application: Shapley-Shubik power index in UN security council. Reading: [MSZ] Chapter 18. [Handwritten notes]

Some project themes

Here some project ideas are given in a cluster. You are free to propose any other project staying within the purview of CS 6001 and 6002. Projects will be evaluated based on the innovation, and if you have independently come up with the project idea, it will be weighed more. Multiple teams can work on different projects under the same theme. It is mandatory to announce with a synopsis of your project on Piazza between January 20-31 (but not before that, use this time to read/ideate on the projects) which project (and theme) your team is working on. Only one post per team (mentioning the team members) is enough. This is to make sure that multiple teams do not start working on the same project.

The themes contain a list of papers that belongs to the same theme. However, they can be different in their approaches. The expectation from the project will be that you read one paper in detail, do a literature survey around it (using the method in the workflow in this page), and attempt to make extensions (either theoretical or practical) on it.

Regrading requests

The regrading procedures in this course are intended to correct serious errors in grading. It is not intended as an opportunity to argue about each judgment call made by the graders. We will only consider a regrading request if there is a significant error in grading, and if you sincerely feel that your exam was unfairly graded, we will look it over carefully. Having said that, if there is an attempt to use this feature to raise an unnecessary regrading request, then based on the severity of the attempt, a penalty will be placed which can go as high as deducting 50% of the marks for that question (irrespective of what marks you got in that question). We are not trying to scare off students whose exams were graded incorrectly, but we are trying to avoid frivolous requests.

What Merits a Regrade: The following are the usual circumstances that may lead to an increase in points:

  • Your answer is really the same as the one on the answer key, but the grader didn’t realize it.
  • Your answer is different from the one provided on the answer key, but your answer is also correct.

What Doesn’t Merit a Regrade: The following are not valid reasons for regrades:

  • “Most of what I wrote is correct, so I think I deserve more partial credit.” Partial credit is given equally for all students who write a particular answer, so it would not be fair to give you more points for this without adding points to all students who wrote the same answer.
  • “I wrote so much, and the grader didn’t notice that the correct answer is buried somewhere within this long paragraph.” You will lose points if the correct answer is accompanied by incorrect information or by so much irrelevant information that it gives the impression that you were just writing down everything you could think of on this topic. To get full credit you must demonstrate the ability to pull out the relevant info and to exclude irrelevant info.
  • “I’m just 1 point away from an A, so I thought it was worth scrounging around to find an extra point somewhere.” Lobbying for grades is considered inappropriate by the institute rules, so your case may be reported to appropriate authorities.
